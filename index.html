About Me

I was a fourth year undergraduate at the University of Chicago majoring in Mathematics, with a concentration in Physics. My general interests include superconductivity, nanofabrication, detector testing, and medical physics. My undergrad resume can be found here.

Research Interests

University of Chicago: MKIDS (November 2017 - June 2020)

Under the direction of Dr. Erik Shirokoff, I fabricate Microwave Kinetic Inductance Detectors (MKIDs) and Kinetic Parametric Up-Converters (KPUPs) to measure quantities of astronomical interest, like CMB anisotropies. My main area of interest is in new material prototyping for later generations of MKID-based devices; current materials of interest are Atomic Layer Deposition (ALD) Titanium Nitride and Niobium Nitride, which each fill different roles for final devices.

  1. Atomic Layer Deposition Titanium Nitride

    Titanium Nitride is an optimal material for fabricating MKIDs and KPUPs because of its high kinetic inductance and tunable critical temperature by the methods described below. In a full device, it forms the basis of the microwave resonators themselves, and we hope to reduce the critical temperature to allow for lower frequency photons to be seen by the detector. Also, its high kinetic inductance allows for larger feature sizes while retaining device performance. In particular, I want to characterize the material properties of ALD Titanium Nitride and use it to make resonators for use in detectors and amplifiers.

  2. Atomic Layer Deposition Niobium Nitride

    ALD Niobium Nitride has the potential to replace Niobium in the transmission lines of the full device due to its much higher critical temperature, around 11K, allowing for higher frequency photons to be transmitted with much less material deposited. Furthermore, it is a promising material for nanowire detectors. ALD Niobium Nitride film thicknesses are around 17nm.

University of Chicago: Medical Physics (September 2019 - June 2020)

Under the supervision of Dr. Patrick La Riviere, I simulate a novel nano-collimator used for 100nm-resolution CT images with the APS beamline at Argonne National Laboratory. We are hoping to fabricate prototype devices later this academic year to judge their future feasibility.

Fermilab: Lee Teng Internship (Summer 2019)

Under the supervision of Dr. Tiziana Spina, I investigate the effects of electron irradiation on Nb and Nb3Sn samples. Nb is a common material used in RF superconducting cavities used in particle accelerators, and Nb3Sn is a novel material for use in superconducting wires and accelerator magnets.

There are three main parts to my project:

  1. Analyzing microscopy images on cavity cutouts, both irradiated and normal, with techniques derived from computer-aided diagnosis literature.
  2. Investigating the superconducting properties - critical temperature, critical current, and critical magnetic field - of Nb3Sn cables using cryo-VSM measurements.
  3. Testing cavity Q vs Eacc performance and temperature mappings before and after irradiation.
